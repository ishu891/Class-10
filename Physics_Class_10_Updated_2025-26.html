20 Competency-Based Questions - Electricity

Q1:
Define electric current and state its SI unit.
Answer:
Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor. It is defined as I = Q/t, where Q is charge in coulombs and t is time in seconds. SI unit is Ampere (A).
Q2:
What is the relationship between conventional current direction and electron flow direction?
Answer:
Conventional current direction is opposite to electron flow direction. Current flows from positive to negative terminal while electrons move from negative to positive terminal.
Q3:
Explain why a material offers resistance to the flow of current.
Answer:
In a conductor, free electrons are attracted by the positively charged nucleus. As they move, they collide with atoms, losing kinetic energy. These collisions resist the flow, creating resistance.
Q4:
State Ohm's law and define resistance.
Answer:
Ohm's law: V = IR, where potential difference V is directly proportional to current I for a constant temperature. Resistance R = V/I is the property of a conductor to oppose current flow.
Q5:
Why is the tungsten used for electric bulb filaments?
Answer:
Tungsten has: (1) very high melting point (~3400¬∞C), (2) high resistivity to produce heat, (3) emits white light at high temperature, (4) low evaporation rate.

Q11:
Why are conductors of electric heating appliances made of alloys rather than pure metals?
Answer:
Alloys have: (1) higher resistivity, (2) higher melting points, (3) better mechanical strength, (4) lower oxidation rates.
Q12:
Explain why series connection is not suitable for domestic circuits.
Answer:
(1) Circuit breaks if one appliance fails, (2) High resistance reduces current, (3) Voltage divided among appliances, (4) Different appliances need different currents.
Q13:
What is the purpose of a fuse in an electrical circuit?
Answer:
A fuse is a safety device that melts when excess current flows due to short circuit or overload, breaking the circuit and preventing damage.
Q14:
Why are copper and aluminum wires preferred for transmission?
Answer:
(1) Low resistivity, (2) High tensile strength, (3) Corrosion resistant, (4) Cost-effective, (5) Good availability.
Q15:
A 40 W bulb and 100 W bulb are connected in parallel to 220 V. Which consumes more energy and why?
Answer:
100 W bulb consumes more. Even though both get same voltage, the 100 W bulb has lower resistance, allowing more current flow.
Q16:
Design a circuit to control the brightness of an electric bulb using a variable resistance.
Answer:
Use rheostat (variable resistance) in series with bulb. As resistance increases, current decreases, bulb becomes dimmer. As resistance decreases, current increases, bulb becomes brighter.
Q17:
Compare series and parallel circuits.
Answer:
Current: Series (same), Parallel (different)
Voltage: Series (divided), Parallel (same)
Applications: Series (old lights), Parallel (household)
Q18:

Q19:
Why might high current at 220 V cause severe shock while 24 V might be safe?
Answer:
Shock severity depends on current, not voltage. At 220 V, body resistance allows dangerous current flow. At 24 V, voltage may not overcome body resistance.
Q20:
What happens when a copper wire is directly connected across 220 V without resistance?
Answer:
Creates short circuit. Huge current flows, generating extreme heat, melting wire, starting fire, and risking electrocution. Fuse prevents this by melting.

20 Competency-Based Questions - Light

Q1:
A ray of light hits a plane mirror at an angle of 30¬∞ with the normal. What will be the angle between the incident ray and the reflected ray?
Answer:
The angle between incident and reflected rays = 180¬∞ - 2(30¬∞) = 120¬∞
Q2:
A light ray enters from air into a glass block. Why does it bend towards the normal?
Answer:
Glass is a denser medium than air. The speed of light decreases in denser medium, causing the ray to bend towards the normal according to Snell's law.
Q3:
Two parallel light rays fall on a concave mirror parallel to its principal axis. After reflection, will they remain parallel? Explain.
Answer:
No. They will converge at the principal focus (F) of the concave mirror because concave mirrors are converging mirrors.
Q4:
A concave mirror forms a real, inverted image of an object. What can you conclude about the position of the object?
Answer:
The object must be placed beyond the center of curvature (C) or between C and F. If the image size equals object size, the object is exactly at C.
Q5:
How does the focal length of a mirror change when it is immersed in water?
Answer:
The focal length remains unchanged. Focal length depends only on the radius of curvature and the mirror's shape, not on the surrounding medium.
Q6:
A convex mirror has a radius of curvature of 40 cm. An object of height 5 cm is placed 30 cm in front of it. Find the position, nature, and size of the image.
Answer:
- Focal length f = R/2 = 20 cm
- Using mirror formula: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u
- 1/20 = 1/v + 1/30
- v = 12 cm (virtual, behind mirror)
- Magnification m = -v/u = -12/(-30) = 0.4
- Image height = 0.4 √ó 5 = 2 cm (erect, diminished)

A light ray traveling in air is incident on a water surface at an angle of 60¬∞ to the normal. If refractive index of water is 1.33, find the angle of refraction.

Q8:
A concave mirror forms a real image which is three times the size of the object. Find the object distance.
Answer:

Q9:
A glass slab of thickness 4 cm and refractive index 1.5 is placed in front of an observer. By how much is the object, placed behind the slab, shifted laterally?
Answer:
This requires specific object distance - the lateral shift depends on angle of incidence.
Q10:
Two lenses have focal lengths f‚ÇÅ = 10 cm and f‚ÇÇ = -15 cm. They are placed in contact. What is the power of the combination?
Answer:
- Power P‚ÇÅ = 1/f‚ÇÅ = 1/0.1 = 10 D

Q11:
Why do stars twinkle while planets do not?
Answer:
Stars are point sources of light. The twinkling is caused by atmospheric refraction which changes continuously. Planets are extended sources, so the variation averages out, preventing twinkling.
Q12:
A concave mirror is used in a solar furnace. Why is this mirror more effective than a convex mirror for this purpose?
Answer:
A concave mirror converges light rays to a focus, concentrating all solar energy at one point. A convex mirror diverges rays, spreading the energy over a large area.
Q13:
An object is placed in front of two different mirrors - one plane and one concave. Both form virtual, erect images. What can you conclude about the object positions?
Answer:
For plane mirror, object can be anywhere. For concave mirror, object must be between pole and focus (between P and F).
Q14:
Explain why a camera and human eye are analogous optical systems.
Answer:
Both have a lens system that forms a real, inverted image on a light-sensitive surface (retina in eye, film/sensor in camera). Both have apertures to control light (iris in eye, aperture in camera).
Q15:
A prism is used to demonstrate dispersion of white light. Why is dispersion more pronounced in a prism than in a glass slab?
Answer:
A prism has non-parallel refracting surfaces inclined to each other, causing different colors to bend at different angles and emerge separated. A glass slab has parallel surfaces, so emergent rays are parallel to incident rays.
Q16:
Design an experiment to determine the focal length of a concave mirror using the sun's rays.
Answer:
Focus sunlight on a paper screen kept at different distances until a bright spot appears. The distance where sharpest spot forms is the focal length (measures ~f = R/2).
Q17:
Explain the phenomenon of rainbow formation using concepts of refraction and dispersion.
Answer:
Water droplets act as prisms. Sunlight refracts into droplets, reflects internally, and refracts again. Different colors refract at different angles. Observer sees these at different heights - VIBGYOR pattern.
Q18:
Why should we avoid looking directly at the sun, even during sunset?
Answer:
The sun's rays can converge on the retina (like a concave mirror focuses light) causing permanent damage to light-sensitive cells, leading to blindness.
Q19:
How can you distinguish between a plane mirror and a very slightly curved concave mirror by looking at the image of your face?
Answer:
For concave mirror very close to face - image is magnified and erect. For plane mirror - image is same size and erect. At larger distances, plane mirror always shows unmagnified image.
Q20:
A thick lens is dropped into water. Will the focal length change? How would the change differ from immersing a thin mirror?
Answer:
Yes, focal length changes (lens-maker's formula contains relative refractive index). For mirror in water, focal length doesn't change (depends only on geometry). This is because lens's focusing depends on refraction (which changes with medium), while mirror's depends on geometry.

15 Competency-Based Questions - Magnetic Effects

Q1:
What is a magnetic field and how do we represent it?
Answer:
A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where magnetic force can be detected. We represent it using magnetic field lines - imaginary curves along which a north pole would move.
Q2:
State the right-hand thumb rule for finding magnetic field direction around a current-carrying conductor.
Answer:
If the right hand thumb points in the direction of current, the fingers curl around the conductor in the direction of magnetic field lines.
Q3:
How does the magnetic field around a solenoid differ from that around a bar magnet?
Answer:
A solenoid produces a field similar to a bar magnet: uniform field inside with parallel lines, field emerges from one end and enters the other.
Q4:
What is an electromagnet and how is it different from a permanent magnet?
Answer:
An electromagnet is a coil wound around soft iron that becomes magnetic when current flows. A permanent magnet retains magnetism. Electromagnet's strength depends on current.
Q5:
Define Fleming's left-hand rule and its application.
Answer:
Stretch thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of left hand mutually perpendicular. First finger = magnetic field, middle finger = current, thumb = force/motion direction.
Q6:
A straight conductor carries 5 A current. At 10 cm perpendicular distance, what is the magnetic field direction if current flows east to west?
Answer:
Using right-hand rule: At 10 cm below wire, field points north; at 10 cm above wire, field points south.

Q10:

Q11:
Explain how an electric bell works using magnetic principles.
Answer:
Current in solenoid creates magnetic field, attracting soft iron armature. Armature moves, striking bell and breaking circuit. Current stops, spring pulls armature back, remakes contact. Cycle repeats rapidly.
Q12:
Why use soft iron core in electromagnets instead of steel?
Answer:
Soft iron loses magnetism quickly when current stops. Steel retains magnetism. Electromagnet needs to switch on/off easily.
Q13:

Ammeter: connect low resistance (shunt) in parallel. Voltmeter: connect high resistance (multiplier) in series.
Q14:

Answer:
Each circular turn produces concentric circles that become parallel lines at center. Combined effect of all turns produces uniform straight parallel field inside.
Q15:
An electron moving horizontally right enters upward magnetic field. Describe its path.
Answer:
The electron follows a circular path due to perpendicular force from magnetic field.

4.3 Atmospheric Phenomena

Twinkling of Stars

Due to atmospheric refraction. Starlight bends continuously through layers of different densities, causing apparent position to fluctuate.

Why planets don't twinkle: They are closer, appear as extended sources (not point sources).

Advance Sunrise & Delayed Sunset

Sun visible 2 minutes before actual sunrise and 2 minutes after sunset due to atmospheric refraction.

Scattering of Light
  • Blue sky: Rayleigh scattering (blue light scattered more)
  • Red sunrise/sunset: Light travels longer path, blue scattered away
  • Danger signals red: Red scatters least, visible from far
  • White clouds: Large water droplets scatter all colors

20 Competency-Based Questions - Human Eye

Q1:
What is the purpose of the iris in the human eye?
Answer:
The iris is a muscular diaphragm that controls the size of the pupil, regulating the amount of light entering the eye. It contracts in bright light and dilates in dim light.
Q2:
Explain the term "power of accommodation" of the eye.
Answer:
It is the ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length by changing its curvature, allowing us to focus on objects at different distances from near point (25 cm) to far point (infinity).
Q3:
What is the near point and far point of a normal human eye?
Answer:
Near point: 25 cm (minimum distance at which objects can be seen distinctly without strain). Far point: infinity (farthest distance at which eye can see objects clearly).
Q4:
A myopic person can see nearby objects clearly but distant objects appear blurred. Explain the defect.
Answer:
In myopia, the eyeball is elongated or the cornea is too curved, causing light rays from distant objects to focus in front of the retina instead of on it.
Q5:
How does a convex lens correct hypermetropia?
Answer:
A convex lens converges light rays, providing additional focusing power. This brings the image of nearby objects forward onto the retina, allowing near vision to be corrected.
Q6:
A myopic person's far point is 100 cm. What should be the power of the corrective lens?
Answer:
- For correction, the lens must form an image at 100 cm for an object at infinity
- Using lens formula: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u
- 1/f = 1/(-100) + 1/‚àû = -1/100
- f = -100 cm = -1 m
- Power P = 1/f = -1 D
Q7:

Answer:
The lens must form an image at 25 cm when object is at 50 cm. For hypermetropia, typically correction gives power ‚âà +2 D (convex lens).
Q8:

Answer:

Q9:
A person suffers from myopia and hypermetropia both (presbyopia). If their powers are -2 D and +1.5 D, what is the bifocal lens power?
Answer:
Bifocal lenses consist of two distinct parts:
1. Upper Part (Distant Vision): Concave lens (-2.0 D).
2. Lower Part (Near Vision): Convex lens (+1.5 D).
The powers are not added; distinct zones are used for different viewing distances.
Q10:
Calculate the focal length of a lens whose power is +2.5 D.
Answer:
- Power P = 1/f
- 2.5 = 1/f
- f = 1/2.5 = 0.4 m = 40 cm (converging/convex lens)
Q11:
Why are blue eyes more sensitive to bright light than brown eyes?
Answer:
Brown eyes have more melanin which absorbs light, providing natural UV protection. Blue eyes have less melanin, allowing more light to reach the retina, making them more sensitive.
Q12:
Explain why the sky appears blue during the day.
Answer:
Blue light has shorter wavelengths and is scattered more by atmospheric particles than other colors (Rayleigh scattering). This scattered blue light reaches our eyes from all directions.
Q13:
Why does the sun appear red during sunrise and sunset?
Answer:
During sunrise/sunset, sunlight travels through a longer path in the atmosphere. Blue light is scattered away, leaving red and orange light to reach the observer.
Q14:
How does the structure of the retina enable color vision?
Answer:
The retina contains two types of cells - cones (for color vision, need bright light) and rods (for black and white vision, work in dim light). Cones contain pigments sensitive to red, green, and blue light.
Q15:
A child has difficulty reading the blackboard from the back of the class. What defect might they have and how should it be corrected?
Answer:
The child likely has myopia (nearsightedness). Distant objects appear blurred. Correction: Use concave lens of appropriate power to diverge light and bring image onto retina.
Q16:
Compare the image formation in the human eye with a camera. What are the similarities and differences?
Answer:
Similarities: Both have lens, aperture (iris/aperture), light-sensitive surface (retina/film). Differences: Eye lens changes shape (accommodation), camera uses different lenses; eye image inverted on retina but brain processes correctly; camera records image.
Q17:
Explain why it's risky to stare at an eclipse, even with protective goggles, if not done carefully.
Answer:
During eclipse, the iris doesn't fully contract due to darkness, but intense focused solar radiation can still reach the retina, causing permanent damage to photoreceptors.
Q18:
Describe the path of light through the human eye and explain the role of each optical component.
Answer:
Light enters through cornea (main refraction ~75%), passes through aqueous humor, iris controls aperture (pupil), light refracted by eye lens (fine focus/accommodation), passes through vitreous humor, forms real, inverted image on retina, retina sends signal via optic nerve to brain.
Q19:
How would a person's vision change if their lens became completely opaque (cataract)?
Answer:
Vision would become progressively blurred and eventually lost because light cannot pass through the lens to reach the retina. This requires cataract surgery to remove and replace the lens.
Q20:
Design a test to determine if a person has color blindness.
Answer:
Use Ishihara color blindness test plates - images with different colored dots forming numbers/patterns visible to normal vision but not to colorblind individuals. This tests specifically for red-green color blindness.
